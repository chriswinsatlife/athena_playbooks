# Turing.com GTM & Marketing Strategy by Channel

**Overview:** Turing (turing.com) is a venture-funded “Talent Cloud” marketplace that connects companies with elite remote software engineers. Backed by over $100M in funding and now valued above $2B ([www.axios.com](https://www.axios.com/newsletters/axios-pro-rata-758978a7-af49-4543-b2c5-7fa1859f35a7#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Turing%2C%20a%20Palo%20Alto%2C,Ventures%2C%20Fortius%20Ventures%2C%20Gaingels%2C%20and)), Turing has evolved its go-to-market to serve both startups and large enterprises. Below is a breakdown of Turing’s current GTM and marketing strategies across key channels, with examples, target audiences, and notable tactics.

## Content Marketing (Blog, Research & Case Studies)  
Turing employs content marketing to engage both sides of its marketplace – *developers* and *hiring managers*. For developers, Turing’s blog (hosted on Medium as “Turingcom”) publishes articles on remote work lifestyle, upskilling, and success stories ([medium.com](https://medium.com/turingcom/turing-com-reviews-from-around-the-world-2022-edition-327c4262a0cd#:~:text=Turingcom%20,5%20min%20readMay%209%2C%202022)) ([medium.com](https://medium.com/turingcom/what-puts-turing-at-the-forefront-of-remote-engineering-talent-d0aef1cdfc9c#:~:text=span%20of%20three%20years%2C%20it,has%20become%20a%20willful%20choice)). For example, one post highlights that in just 3 years Turing became “a willful choice of over half a million remote engineers worldwide” by helping them land dream jobs from home ([medium.com](https://medium.com/turingcom/what-puts-turing-at-the-forefront-of-remote-engineering-talent-d0aef1cdfc9c#:~:text=span%20of%20three%20years%2C%20it,has%20become%20a%20willful%20choice)). This kind of content (often written by Turing’s team or community) reinforces Turing’s appeal to global tech talent. 

For prospective *clients* (startups to enterprises), Turing produces more formal resources like whitepapers, playbooks, and case studies. In 2022, Turing partnered with research firm Zinnov on a whitepaper defining the “Intelligent Talent Cloud” – showcasing how deep-tech and AI can build/manage remote teams ([www.prnewswire.com](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/new-white-paper-on-the-future-of-remote-work-announces-the-rise-of-the-intelligent-talent-cloud-301535573.html#:~:text=Intelligent%20Talent%20Cloud%20in%20a,increase%20in%20demand%20for%20digital)). They also create industry-specific guides: for instance, Turing’s **“GenAI Playbook to Conversational Commerce in Retail”** is a downloadable ebook on using generative AI in retail, offered as a lead magnet on their site ([go.turing.com](https://go.turing.com/resources/conversational-commerce-playbook#:~:text=Turing%20go,cart)). Another example is the **“GenAI Playbook to Retail Asset Creation”**, which promises insights into integrating generative AI for faster content production in marketing ([go.turing.com](https://go.turing.com/resources/retail-asset-creation-playbook#:~:text=Ready%20to%20Accelerate%20Retail%20Asset,Creation)). These in-depth playbooks (gated behind a download form) position Turing as a thought leader in applying AI with remote talent – a strategy aimed at enterprise product and engineering leaders exploring new tech. 

Turing’s website and sales materials also highlight **customer success stories**. Some of these are private or shared upon request (the “Case Studies” section requires sign-in), but Turing does publicize key metrics and logos. For example, marketing collateral notes that *“400+ top companies, including Pepsi, Johnson & Johnson, and Dell, trust Turing's Talent Cloud for their engineering needs.”* ([hackernoon.com](https://hackernoon.com/company/turing#:~:text=Turing%20enables%3A%20,Talent%20Cloud%20matches%20you%20to)) ([hackernoon.com](https://hackernoon.com/company/turing#:~:text=the%20world%20400%2B%20top%20companies%2C,Talent%20Cloud%20matches%20you%20to)) This social proof signals to enterprise prospects that Fortune 500 firms already rely on Turing. In summary, the content mix ranges from blog posts and Medium articles (targeted at developers and engineering managers) to heavyweight reports and case studies (targeted at HR/tech executives in larger firms). The unique tactics here include **playbook-style content** tied to hot trends (AI) and showcasing big-name client outcomes – all designed to educate the market and capture leads.

## SEO and Organic Acquisition (Keywords & Content)  
Turing drives organic acquisition by targeting keywords around *remote hiring* and *software talent*. Their site copy emphasizes terms like “vetted remote developers” and “Silicon Valley-standard engineers,” which align with popular search queries for hiring quality developers abroad ([help.turing.com](https://help.turing.com/hc/en-us/articles/4403600749455-why-should-i-hire-remote-developers-from-turing-#:~:text=Turing%20offers%20high,manage%201M%2B%20software%20developers%20worldwide)). Turing likely maintains landing pages or sections for specific skill domains (e.g. “Hire Remote Python Developers” or “Hire React Developers”), though much of the detailed content might be behind a sign-up. In practice, Turing leverages content and third-party platforms for SEO visibility. For instance, they’ve worked with tech publications – a HackerNoon company profile describes how *“Turing combines AI and a global reach to deliver your ideal engineers tailored to your business needs”* ([hackernoon.com](https://hackernoon.com/company/turing#:~:text=Turing%20enables%3A%20,Talent%20Cloud%20matches%20you%20to)), reinforcing their keywords and providing backlinks. Similarly, Turing’s own Medium articles and guest posts on relevant blogs improve their search footprint for topics like “managing remote developers” or “best platforms to hire remote engineers.” 

**Core keyword clusters** for Turing likely include “hire remote developers,” “remote software engineers,” and “[skill] developer remote.” By creating content that answers these queries (e.g. guides to hire remote devs, comparison pieces, etc.), Turing captures inbound interest from startups and SMBs searching for talent solutions. SEO is probably less crucial for engaging known enterprises (who are reached more via direct sales), but it brings in a steady stream of smaller tech companies and individual hiring managers. Turing’s **programmatic SEO** efforts are not overt (they don’t list thousands of public profiles as some job boards do), yet their broad footprint of 1M+ developers across 140+ countries ([help.turing.com](https://help.turing.com/hc/en-us/articles/4403600749455-why-should-i-hire-remote-developers-from-turing-#:~:text=Turing%20offers%20high,manage%201M%2B%20software%20developers%20worldwide)) naturally yields long-tail organic presence (e.g. developers sharing “working with Turing” on forums, or articles on remote work referencing Turing). They also ensure high Google relevance by publishing authoritative content – e.g. a detailed FAQ explaining *“why [to] hire remote developers from Turing”* highlights their AI-driven platform and global reach ([help.turing.com](https://help.turing.com/hc/en-us/articles/4403600749455-why-should-i-hire-remote-developers-from-turing-#:~:text=Turing%20offers%20high,manage%201M%2B%20software%20developers%20worldwide)), which aligns with frequent search questions. Overall, SEO for Turing is about owning the conversation on remote hiring and being discovered by companies looking to scale engineering teams cost-effectively.

## Paid Acquisition (Search, Social & Retargeting)  
With their significant funding, Turing invests heavily in paid acquisition to accelerate growth. On **search engines**, Turing likely bids on high-intent keywords related to hiring developers (e.g. “hire remote developers”, “hire software engineer fast”). Companies searching these terms will often see Turing’s ads alongside competitors like Toptal or Upwork. The goal is to capture those ready-to-hire leads by highlighting Turing’s key benefits in ad copy – for example, “Vetted Silicon Valley-caliber Engineers in 3 Days” or “Hire Risk-Free (2-week trial) ([hrmanagement.saastrac.com](https://hrmanagement.saastrac.com/turing-review/#:~:text=They%20have%20a%20very%20rigid,before%20committing%20to%20hiring%20them)).” Indeed, Turing offers a **2-week risk-free trial** for new clients to try a developer, which is a compelling hook they emphasize in marketing ([hrmanagement.saastrac.com](https://hrmanagement.saastrac.com/turing-review/#:~:text=They%20have%20a%20very%20rigid,before%20committing%20to%20hiring%20them)). This trial feature appears in many reviews and likely in ad messages to reduce friction for first-time customers.

On **social media ads**, Turing uses platforms like LinkedIn and Facebook for targeting. LinkedIn is invaluable for B2B: Turing can target startup founders, CTOs, engineering managers, or HR directors with sponsored posts about “scaling your dev team 10x faster” or similar value props. Given Turing’s massive LinkedIn following (over 850k followers on its page as of recent counts) ([pu.linkedin.com](https://pu.linkedin.com/company/turingcom#:~:text=Turing%20,is%20one%20of%20the%20world%E2%80%99s)), it’s evident they’ve built brand awareness there – likely through both organic content and paid promotions. Ads on LinkedIn could promote their whitepapers or webinars (to capture enterprise leads) or simply drive traffic to “Book a Demo.” On Facebook/Instagram, Turing may target more of the developer side or smaller businesses. For example, they might run campaigns in regions with strong developer communities (India, Latin America) to sign up talent, using ads that tout “USA remote jobs, earn in USD” – a message that resonates with developers (their blog even has posts about earning in USD remotely ([medium.com](https://medium.com/turingcom/turing-com-reviews-from-around-the-world-2022-edition-327c4262a0cd#:~:text=Turing,5%20min%20readMay%209%2C%202022))). For client acquisition though, LinkedIn and Google are probably the primary paid channels.

**Retargeting** is another piece: Visitors who check Turing’s site or content are likely followed by banner ads reminding them of Turing’s services. Someone who browsed the “Hire developers” page might later see an ad on the Google Display Network or LinkedIn saying “Still looking for engineers? Try Turing’s pre-vetted talent cloud.” These nurture touches keep Turing top-of-mind. Unique tactics in paid acquisition include advertising the *speed and quality guarantee* (e.g. “hire in days, Silicon Valley-quality”) and the **trial period** as mentioned. Turing’s deep funding allows a sizeable budget for these paid channels, which is crucial in a market where competitors also aggressively advertise. By appearing wherever a potential customer might be researching (Google search) or networking (LinkedIn), Turing ensures it captures demand at multiple stages of the buyer journey.

## Sales-Led Motion (Enterprise Sales, Outbound & Demos)  
While self-service is possible, Turing has a strong **sales-led motion** for enterprise and high-value clients. The company has built a direct sales force and SDR/BDR team that actively reach out to target accounts. Turing’s *target customers* include leading AI labs, Fortune 500 companies, and high-growth tech startups across sectors like finance, retail, and healthcare ([canvasbusinessmodel.com](https://canvasbusinessmodel.com/blogs/marketing-strategy/turing-marketing-strategy#:~:text=The%20sales%20channels%20of%20the,challenges%20and%20offering%20tailored%20solutions)). This means a lot of their bigger deals come through relationship-based selling rather than purely inbound signup. Their sales strategy is **consultative** – SDRs or account executives engage prospects to understand talent gaps, then propose how Turing’s vetted engineers can solve those specific challenges ([canvasbusinessmodel.com](https://canvasbusinessmodel.com/blogs/marketing-strategy/turing-marketing-strategy#:~:text=The%20sales%20channels%20of%20the,challenges%20and%20offering%20tailored%20solutions)) ([canvasbusinessmodel.com](https://canvasbusinessmodel.com/blogs/marketing-strategy/turing-marketing-strategy#:~:text=The%20core%20offering%20is%20an,has%20significantly%20impacted%20its%20growth)). For example, an enterprise with a cloud migration project might be approached with a tailored pitch that Turing can provide an entire team of AWS-certified developers quickly.

Turing’s core offering for sales to push is an **AI-powered platform with over 4 million developers** available ([canvasbusinessmodel.com](https://canvasbusinessmodel.com/blogs/marketing-strategy/turing-marketing-strategy#:~:text=The%20core%20offering%20is%20an,has%20significantly%20impacted%20its%20growth)). Sales reps emphasize that with this vast pool and AI matching, Turing enables *“rapid deployment of talent”* on long-term engagements ([canvasbusinessmodel.com](https://canvasbusinessmodel.com/blogs/marketing-strategy/turing-marketing-strategy#:~:text=The%20core%20offering%20is%20an,has%20significantly%20impacted%20its%20growth)). In late 2022, Turing even launched a new “**Dedicated Teams**” service to appeal to enterprises needing not just one engineer but a full, integrated team for complex projects ([aithority.com](https://aithority.com/machine-learning/turing-launches-new-teams-offering-to-provide-enterprises-with-a-complete-dedicated-development-team-solution-for-complex-engineering-problems/#:~:text=Turing%20Launches%20New%20Teams%20Offering,Solution%20for%20Complex%20Engineering%20Problems)). This move (essentially offering a turnkey development team) shows how the sales motion has evolved to capture larger, consultative deals – more akin to IT services/outsourcing contracts, but with Turing’s remote talent twist. In selling this, Turing’s team will conduct **demos** of the platform (showing how a client can browse vetted candidates, monitor progress, etc.), share **case studies**, and often pilot a small engagement. The free trial period is a classic “land and expand” tactic: a VP of Engineering can start with one trial developer, and once convinced of the quality, Turing’s sales team works to expand that into multiple hires or a whole offshore team.

Outbound prospecting and **Account-Based Marketing (ABM)** are likely employed for big accounts. Turing’s sales/marketing might identify, say, a Fortune 100 company struggling to hire AI engineers and then run targeted LinkedIn ads, personalized emails, or even send the company a custom “playbook” addressing their situation. Because Turing’s platform addresses a timely pain point (tech talent shortage, need for AI expertise), their outbound messages focus on ROI: e.g. “reduce time-to-hire from 3 months to 1 week” or “access niche skills (AI/ML, data engineering) that are scarce locally.” They also leverage **social proof in sales decks** – citing companies like Johnson & Johnson or OpenAI as clients – to build credibility. According to one strategy analysis, Turing’s shift from just staff augmentation to also offering full **technology consulting services** in 2023 led to a 70% surge in client base, demonstrating the success of their expanded sales approach ([canvasbusinessmodel.com](https://canvasbusinessmodel.com/blogs/marketing-strategy/turing-marketing-strategy#:~:text=services%2C%20launched%20in%20March%202023%2C,has%20significantly%20impacted%20its%20growth)). In summary, Turing’s sales-led motion is characterized by a hands-on, high-touch approach: *outbound outreach, consultative solution selling, demos/pilots, and enterprise account management*. This is aimed at winning long-term commitments from enterprises and fast-scaling startups, rather than just one-off gig placements.
